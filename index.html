Purpose

This project, ‘Distortion Playground’, aims to create a browser-based interactive expression tool. Its core objective is to redefine the user’s role in digital creation—shifting from the traditional ‘image producer’ to a ‘spatial manipulator’. Unlike conventional drawing tools, which are geared towards generating visual outcomes, this tool guides users to interact directly with the digital interface itself, treating the web page as a dynamic medium that can be stretched, distorted and reconfigured.

In three preliminary interaction experiments, I examined the relationship between user input and system feedback from multiple perspectives. Firstly, in a basic piano interface experiment, I labelled three sliders: ‘Volume’, ‘Synth’ and ‘Tone’. By adjusting the sliders' colour scheme, adding icons on either side, and providing clear labels, I enabled users to quickly grasp the direction and meaning of parameter changes at a glance. Secondly, in the button feedback experiment, I tested interface state changes through click and hover behaviours: when the user clicked the ‘click me’ button, it changed from yellow to pink, whilst the page background shifted from white to green; when the mouse hovered over the button, it turned orange. This experiment highlighted the role of immediate visual feedback in the interactive experience. Finally, in the mapping experiment, I configured the interface so that when users clicked a button, circles of random sizes and positions would appear against a pale yellow background, limited to three distinct shades of blue, thereby exploring how discrete inputs are transformed into perceptible visual outcomes.

These experiments demonstrate that users can form an immediate understanding when a perceptible connection is established between their behaviour and the system’s feedback. Consequently, this project regards ‘interaction’ as an embodied experience, emphasising the role of physical movement in the process of expression.

Furthermore, this project draws inspiration from creative tools such as Broider and Wigglypaint. The former generates complex patterns through minimal input, whilst the latter subverts the precision of traditional drawing through unstable lines. Together, these tools have inspired me to consider that expression does not depend on the stability of control, but can arise from the randomness of the system. Consequently, this project extends this line of thinking to the ‘spatial dimension’.

Worth

The value of Distortion Playground lies not in its functionality or productivity, but in the aesthetic experience and space for reflection it offers users as an exploratory interactive tool. Unlike efficiency-driven software, this project is closer to an experimental digital art tool, its significance lying in stimulating users’ perception and understanding of the interactive process itself.

Firstly, by mapping user gestures directly onto spatial deformations, the project enhances the role of feedback within the interactive experience. Immediate visual responses not only heighten the sense of engagement but also give users a greater sense of control over the system’s behaviour. However, at the same time, the uncertainty introduced by features such as ‘Heat’ mode undermines complete controllability, thereby creating a tension between ‘control’ and ‘loss of control’. This tension prompts users to rethink the stability of digital systems.

In my independent research, I analysed WeaveSilk and Patatap. The former generates exquisite artistic patterns through symmetry and fluid motion using simple gestures, whilst the latter enhances interactivity and playability through rapid graphic animations and audiovisual feedback. These tools are outcome-oriented. In contrast, this project places greater emphasis on the ‘process of transformation’ itself, thereby offering a different perspective on interaction.

Overall, the value of this project lies on two levels: firstly, at the experiential level, enabling users to perceive the malleability of digital space; and secondly, at the conceptual level, challenging established perceptions of interface stability and exploring the expressive potential of interaction design.

Framing

The design framework of Distortion Playground is deliberately kept simple, using a limited set of functions to create a clear and exploratory interactive system. At its core lies a visual space based on a regular grid, which also serves as the object of user interaction.

The system offers three deformation modes: Liquid, Elastic and Heat. The Liquid mode emphasises smooth, continuous flow effects; the Elastic mode incorporates a rebound mechanism; whilst the Heat mode simulates air refraction through unstable ripples. These three modes provide users with a variety of experiences whilst maintaining a simple technical implementation.

Interaction is primarily based on mouse or touch controls, supplemented by adjustments to parameters such as intensity, range and speed. These controls are not merely functional settings; they also serve as a visual representation of the ‘mapping relationship’, enabling users to understand how their actions affect the system.

Technically, the project utilises HTML, CSS and JavaScript, achieving visual transformations by manipulating the positions of grid points rather than through complex pixel-level processing. This approach both reduces development complexity and enhances the expression of artist variation.Overall, the design framework strikes a balance between simplicity and expressiveness, making the tool both easy to use and offering a certain depth of exploration.

Conceptual Response

Creative context: Put simply, the starting point for this project is a re-examination of traditional digital drawing tools. Tools such as Photoshop or Illustrator are underpinned by a logic of precise control—users draw lines, fill in colours and adjust anchor points, with every step leading to a predetermined outcome. There is nothing inherently wrong with this logic, but it assumes that creation is a direct process moving from intention to execution. The problem is that many interesting visual outcomes arise precisely from moments of loss of control, yet traditional tools virtually rule out this possibility.

What this tool aims to do is transform ‘loss of control’ into a state that can be actively chosen. Here, the user is not manipulating a brush, but an existing spatial structure. A grid is pre-existing on the canvas; by swiping, the user pushes and distorts it, altering the form of the entire field rather than adding new elements to a blank space. This distinction may seem minor, but it fundamentally changes the relationship between the user and the canvas—the user is no longer a ‘producer’, but rather interacts with an existing system.

This tool is primarily aimed at two types of users: ordinary people interested in artistic creation but lacking a background in drawing, and creators seeking to explore new forms of expression. Neither group requires any technical expertise to get started. The generated grid patterns can be exported as PNG files via the ‘Save’ function and inserted into posters or other media for further use; thus, it is not merely a tool for ‘play’, but also serves a practical creative purpose.

Interaction design principle: In terms of interaction design principles, this project centres on ‘mapping’. Norman (2013) notes that good mapping involves a natural correspondence between the controller’s actions and the system’s responses. This tool applies this principle to the spatial dimension: the direction of the user’s mouse movement directly corresponds to the direction of the grid’s movement; wherever the user drags, that area deforms, with no intermediate steps. This correspondence is entirely intuitive, requiring no learning on the user’s part. The three distortion modes offer varying degrees of unpredictability. The behaviour of the Liquid and Elastic modes is relatively predictable, whereas the Heat mode introduces time-based random fluctuations, resulting in the system exhibiting its own ‘mind’ alongside the user’s actions. This design philosophy is similar to that of certain tools in the generative art field, such as WeaveSilk and Patatap, which all emphasise the value of chance and the process of change itself, rather than whether the final image meets expectations.

Technical Approach

At the heart of this tool is a grid-based physics system running on an HTML5 Canvas. When the page loads, the `createGrid()` function generates a uniform grid on the canvas with 40px spacing; each point stores its current coordinates, initial coordinates and velocity vectors (vx, vy). The initial coordinates allow the ‘Reset’ function to immediately return all points to their starting positions without the need for recalculation. The canvas dimensions are determined by resizeCanvas(), which reads the actual size after CSS rendering (getBoundingClientRect()) and writes it to the canvas’s pixel buffer, ensuring that mouse coordinates and point coordinates remain aligned within the same space under a 4:3 aspect ratio.

User gestures are converted into physical responses via `applyDistortion()`. This function applies an outward velocity impulse to every point within a 120px radius, with the force calculated using a quadratic decay formula (`Math.pow(1 - dist / radius, 2)`), such that the thrust is greater the closer the point is to the mouse. The three modes adjust the multiplier based on this: Liquid uses ×2.5, Elastic uses ×6.0, and Heat uses ×1.5 plus a lateral noise term of Math. sin(Date.now() * 0.02 + p.y * 0.1) * 0.2. During rapid movement, there are gaps between pointermove events; the code uses linear interpolation to interpolate points between the positions of two frames, ensuring the distorted trajectory remains continuous. The rendering loop is driven by `requestAnimationFrame`; each frame, the velocity is multiplied by 0.9 to apply decay, causing the point to naturally decelerate and come to rest. The export function uses `canvas.toDataURL(‘image/png’)` to capture the current frame’s pixels and triggers a download via a temporary `a` tag, without relying on any external libraries. For further technical details, please refer to the MDN Canvas API documentation (MDN Web Docs, n.d.-a) and the Pointer Events specification (MDN Web Docs, n.d.-b).

Peer User Testing Reflection

This test yielded 17 responses. Overall, there were several consistent findings and some unexpected results.

In terms of usage patterns, the most common action for the majority of users was to simply drag the mouse to distort the grid, rather than repeatedly switching modes or adjusting the intensity. This suggests that the tool’s core interaction—where gestures map to spatial deformation—is intuitive enough for users to pick up without much learning. However, this also highlights a problem: if users consistently rely on the dragging action, the differences between the modes become difficult to perceive. Several participants explicitly mentioned that Liquid and Elastic looked quite similar, a concern I had briefly harboured during development. Ironically, the Heat mode—due to the addition of random jitter—proved to be the most easily distinguishable of the three, and was the most frequently selected. This result led me to consider whether the force multipliers for Liquid and Elastic should be set further apart.

Regarding the scope for creative expression, the feedback revealed a fairly clear divide. Some users felt the tool offered sufficient creative scope, believing it could be used to express emotions or create assets; others felt that the results always looked similar, lacking a sense of personalisation. This divergence is actually quite interesting—the tool deliberately downplays ‘precise control’, yet this acts as a limitation for some users. It made me realise that ‘unpredictability’ as a design intent is not something everyone will naturally accept; the tool may need to provide users with a few more fixed functions in certain areas.

There are two specific issues raised in the feedback that warrant addressing. Firstly, the background of the saved image is transparent, which differs from the beige background seen on the webpage; some users feel the exported result is quite different from what they expected. This issue is not difficult to resolve technically; simply drawing the background colour onto the canvas before exporting will do the trick, and I have included this in the full version. Secondly, when the intensity slider is set to its lowest point, it remains completely stationary. Some users felt this state served no purpose and suggested retaining a minimal amount of movement. This feedback is entirely reasonable; a slight response even at extremely low intensities would create a more seamless user experience.

One rather unique suggestion was to introduce an ‘attraction mode’—where the mouse pulls points towards it rather than pushing them away. This is the opposite of the current repulsion logic, but it is entirely feasible technically; it simply requires reversing the direction of the force. It’s a rather good idea, as it would offer users a completely different feel to the interaction, and I’m keen to try it out as a separate, switchable feature.
